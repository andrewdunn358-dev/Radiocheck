1. Overview

Welcome to Radio Check! This manual will help you provide effective support to UK military veterans through our platform.

What is Radio Check?

Radio Check is a 24/7 mental health and peer support platform designed specifically for UK military veterans. Veterans can access support through:

  • AI "Battle Buddies" - AI chat companions with military-aware personas
  • Live Chat - Real-time text chat with counsellors and peer supporters
  • Callback Requests - Request a call from a staff member
  • Crisis Support - Emergency resources and panic button

Your Role

Counsellor

Provide clinical support, manage cases, handle complex situations

Peer Supporter

Offer peer support, share lived experience, escalate when needed

Supervisor

Manage team, handle escalations, provide supervision

2. Getting Started

Logging In

  1. Navigate to the Staff Portal URL (staffportal.radiocheck.me)
  2. Enter your email address
  3. Enter your password
  4. Click "Login"
First Time Login

If this is your first login, you will have received a temporary password by email. You should change this immediately via your profile settings.

Portal Layout

The Staff Portal has several tabs:

  • Dashboard - Overview of your status and pending items
  • Live Chat - Handle incoming chat requests
  • Callbacks - View and manage callback requests
  • Safeguarding - View and respond to safeguarding alerts
  • Supervision - (Supervisors only) Team management and notes

3. Setting Your Status

Status Options

Your status tells veterans and colleagues whether you're available:

  • Available - Ready to accept chats and calls
  • Busy - Currently engaged, won't receive new requests
  • Off - Not on shift, not receiving requests

Changing Your Status

  1. Look for the status dropdown at the top of the dashboard
  2. Click to open the dropdown
  3. Select your new status
  4. The change takes effect immediately
Important

Always set your status to "Available" when starting your shift and "Off" when ending. Don't leave yourself as "Available" when not actively monitoring.

4. Live Chat

Receiving Chat Requests

When a veteran requests a live chat and you're available:

  1. You'll receive a notification (sound + visual alert)
  2. The chat request will appear in your Live Chat tab
  3. Click Accept to join the chat

During the Chat

  • Introduce yourself briefly
  • Listen actively and respond with empathy
  • Use the veteran's name if they provide it
  • Avoid jargon - use plain, accessible language
  • Take your time - there's no rush
Chat Tips

Remember that text chat lacks tone of voice. Be explicit about your empathy: "That sounds really difficult" rather than just "I understand".

Ending a Chat

  1. Check if the veteran has any remaining questions
  2. Summarise any agreed actions
  3. Offer follow-up options if appropriate
  4. Click End Chat when both parties are ready
  5. Complete the chat summary/notes

5. Handling Callbacks

Callback Requests

Veterans can request a callback through the app. When a request comes in:

  1. Review the request details (name, preferred time, reason)
  2. Click Claim to assign yourself to the callback
  3. Call the veteran at the requested time
  4. After the call, record the outcome

Making the Call

  • Use the in-app calling feature (via WebRTC) when possible
  • Introduce yourself: "Hi, this is [Name] from Radio Check..."
  • Confirm you're speaking to the right person
  • Ask if it's still a good time to talk

Recording Outcomes

After each callback, record:

  • Whether the call was completed
  • Brief summary of the conversation
  • Any follow-up actions needed
  • Safeguarding concerns (if any)

6. Safeguarding

Recognising Risk

Be alert to signs of:

  • Suicidal ideation - Expressions of wanting to end life, feeling hopeless
  • Self-harm - Current or intended self-injury
  • Risk to others - Threats of violence or harm
  • Safeguarding concerns - Abuse, neglect, vulnerable adults/children
If Someone is in Immediate Danger

If you believe someone is at immediate risk of harm:

  1. Stay calm and keep talking to them
  2. Ask directly: "Are you safe right now?"
  3. If they share location, note it
  4. Alert your supervisor immediately
  5. If needed, contact emergency services (999)

Safeguarding Alerts

The system automatically flags high-risk keywords in chats. When an alert is triggered:

  1. Review the flagged content
  2. Assess the level of risk
  3. Take appropriate action
  4. Document your response

Risk Levels

  • High Risk - Immediate danger, requires urgent action
  • Medium Risk - Concerning, requires follow-up within 24 hours
  • Low Risk - Monitor, document for future reference

7. Escalation

When to Escalate

As a Peer Supporter, escalate to a Counsellor or Supervisor when:

  • The veteran needs clinical support beyond peer support
  • You're unsure how to handle a situation
  • There are safeguarding concerns
  • The veteran requests to speak to a counsellor
  • You feel out of your depth
It's OK to Escalate

Escalating is a sign of good judgement, not failure. Knowing your limits protects both you and the veteran.

How to Escalate

  1. Click the Escalate button in the chat or dashboard
  2. Select the reason for escalation
  3. Add any relevant notes
  4. If urgent, use the Panic Button feature

Panic Button

The Panic Button is for urgent situations that need immediate supervisor attention. Use it when:

  • You're in a chat with someone at immediate risk
  • You need urgent backup
  • The situation is beyond your capability

8. Supervision (Supervisors Only)

Team Management

As a Supervisor, you can:

  • View all counsellors and peer supporters
  • See their current status and workload
  • Review their chat history and case notes
  • Handle escalations directed to you

1:1 Supervision Notes

Record supervision sessions with your team:

  1. Go to the Supervision tab
  2. Select the staff member
  3. Click Add Supervision Note
  4. Record discussion points, agreed actions, and any concerns
  5. Mark as confidential if HR-sensitive

Handling Escalations

When a team member escalates to you:

  1. Review the escalation details
  2. Acknowledge the escalation
  3. Take over the case if needed, or provide guidance
  4. Record the resolution

9. Case Management (Counsellors)

Opening a Case

For veterans who need ongoing support:

  1. Go to Case Management
  2. Click New Case
  3. Enter veteran details and presenting issues
  4. Set the case priority and assign to yourself

Case Notes

Keep detailed, professional notes for each interaction:

  • Date and time of contact
  • Method of contact (chat, call, etc.)
  • Summary of discussion
  • Risk assessment
  • Agreed actions and follow-up

Closing a Case

When closing a case, record:

  • Reason for closure
  • Outcome summary
  • Any ongoing risks
  • Referrals made

10. Best Practices

Communication

  • Listen more than you speak - Let the veteran lead
  • Validate feelings - "That sounds really difficult"
  • Avoid judgement - No matter what they share
  • Use military-aware language - Understand forces culture
  • Be genuine - Veterans can spot insincerity

Self-Care

Supporting veterans can be emotionally demanding. Remember to:

  • Take breaks between difficult conversations
  • Use supervision for debriefing
  • Know your limits and escalate when needed
  • Look after your own mental health
  • Speak up if you're struggling
You Matter Too

You can't pour from an empty cup. If you're struggling after a difficult conversation, speak to your supervisor. There's no shame in needing support.

11. Emergency Procedures

Emergency Contacts

  • Emergency Services: 999
  • Samaritans: 116 123
  • Combat Stress: 0800 138 1619
  • Veterans Gateway: 0808 802 1212

If You Need to Contact Emergency Services

  1. Stay calm and keep the veteran talking if in chat
  2. Try to get their location (the app may have geolocation)
  3. Alert your supervisor immediately
  4. Call 999 if immediate danger
  5. Provide all information to emergency services
  6. Document everything afterwards
When to Call 999
  • Someone is actively harming themselves
  • Someone has taken an overdose
  • Someone is threatening to harm others
  • Someone has a plan and means for suicide
  • You believe life is at immediate risk
